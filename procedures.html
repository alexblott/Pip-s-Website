Endometriosis & Pelvic Pain

Understanding endometriosis, chronic pelvic pain, and your treatment options

The endometrium is the tissue that normally lines the inside of the uterus (womb) and is shed each month during your period. Endometriosis occurs when similar tissue grows outside the uterus — most commonly on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, bowel, bladder, and the lining of the pelvis (peritoneum).

This tissue responds to your hormonal cycle in the same way the uterine lining does, causing bleeding, inflammation, and scarring in areas where it doesn't belong. Over time this can lead to pain, scar tissue (adhesions), and cysts on the ovaries (endometriomas).

Endometriosis is common, affecting around 1 in 7–10 women and people assigned female at birth during their reproductive years. It is a leading cause of chronic pelvic pain and can also affect fertility, but many people with endometriosis conceive without difficulty.

Common symptoms
  • Painful periods (dysmenorrhoea) that may worsen over time or not respond well to standard pain relief
  • Chronic pelvic pain, present even outside of your period
  • Pain with intercourse (dyspareunia), particularly with deep penetration
  • Pain with opening your bowels or passing urine, especially around your period
  • Heavy or irregular menstrual bleeding
  • Bloating, fatigue, and low mood associated with flare-ups
  • Difficulty conceiving
  • Some people have no symptoms at all, and endometriosis is found incidentally
What causes it?

The exact cause of endometriosis isn't fully understood. Leading theories include retrograde menstruation (menstrual blood flowing backwards through the fallopian tubes), changes in immune function that prevent the body clearing this tissue, and possible genetic and hormonal factors.

It tends to run in families, and having a first-degree relative with endometriosis increases your own risk.

How it's diagnosed

Endometriosis is suspected based on your symptoms and history, supported by a pelvic examination and imaging.

  • Pelvic ultrasound — particularly a specialised 'endometriosis protocol' ultrasound — can identify ovarian endometriomas and some deep disease
  • MRI may be used in more complex cases to map disease before surgery
  • Laparoscopy (keyhole surgery) remains the only way to definitively diagnose and, at the same time, treat endometriosis by directly visualising and, where appropriate, excising or biopsying affected tissue

A normal scan does not exclude endometriosis — imaging cannot reliably detect superficial disease, which laparoscopy can identify.

Treatment options

Medical management

  • Regular pain relief (e.g. anti-inflammatories) used strategically around your cycle
  • Hormonal treatments to suppress ovulation and periods — combined oral contraceptive pill, progestogen-only options, or the hormonal IUD (Mirena)
  • GnRH analogues for more persistent symptoms, used under specialist supervision

Surgical management

  • Laparoscopic excision of endometriosis — removal of visible disease, which can improve pain and fertility outcomes
  • Treatment of endometriomas (ovarian cysts related to endometriosis) with attention to preserving ovarian reserve
  • Multidisciplinary surgery for deep infiltrating endometriosis involving bowel or bladder, where required

Supportive care

  • Pelvic floor physiotherapy
  • Pain psychology and multidisciplinary chronic pain approaches for persistent symptoms
  • Dietary and lifestyle strategies some patients find helpful alongside medical care

Dr Robertson has undertaken additional training in advanced laparoscopic excision of endometriosis and will work with you to individualise a management plan based on your symptoms, disease severity, and fertility goals.

When to seek prompt medical attention
• Sudden, severe pelvic pain, particularly with fever, dizziness, or fainting
• Heavy bleeding soaking a pad or tampon every hour
• Pain that is significantly worse than your usual pattern
• Any concern that is affecting your ability to function — please contact the rooms rather than waiting for your next review

Fibroids

Uterine leiomyomas — what they are and how they're managed

Fibroids (also called uterine leiomyomas or myomas) are benign (non-cancerous) growths that develop from the muscle wall of the uterus. They are extremely common — by age 50, up to 70–80% of women will have at least one fibroid, though many cause no symptoms at all.

Fibroids vary widely in size, number, and location. They may grow within the muscle wall (intramural), bulge into the uterine cavity (submucosal), or project from the outer surface of the uterus (subserosal). Their location has a big influence on the symptoms they cause and the treatment options available.

Common symptoms
  • Heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding
  • Pelvic pressure, heaviness, or a palpable lump/swelling in the lower abdomen
  • Pelvic or lower back pain
  • Frequent urination or a feeling of incomplete bladder emptying, if fibroids press on the bladder
  • Pain or discomfort during intercourse
  • Difficulty conceiving or pregnancy complications, in some cases
  • Many fibroids are asymptomatic and found incidentally on examination or imaging
What causes it?

Fibroids are hormonally driven — they grow in response to oestrogen and progesterone and typically shrink after menopause.

Risk factors include family history, being of reproductive age, earlier onset of periods, and being of African or African-Caribbean descent, who tend to develop fibroids earlier and more frequently.

How it's diagnosed

Fibroids are often identified on a routine pelvic examination and confirmed with imaging.

  • Pelvic ultrasound is usually the first-line and most accessible test
  • MRI can provide more detailed mapping, particularly before surgery, or when there are multiple or large fibroids
  • Hysteroscopy allows direct visualisation and, in some cases, treatment of fibroids bulging into the uterine cavity
Treatment options

Watchful waiting

  • If fibroids are small and not causing symptoms, no treatment may be needed — just routine monitoring

Medical management

  • Tranexamic acid or anti-inflammatories to reduce heavy bleeding
  • Hormonal treatments including the combined pill, progestogens, or the hormonal IUD
  • GnRH analogues to shrink fibroids and reduce bleeding, typically used short-term or before surgery

Surgical and procedural options

  • Hysteroscopic resection for fibroids within the uterine cavity — a day-case procedure with quick recovery
  • Laparoscopic or open myomectomy — removal of fibroids while preserving the uterus, an option for those wishing to retain fertility
  • Hysterectomy — definitive treatment where fertility preservation isn't required and symptoms are significant
  • Uterine artery embolisation — a radiological alternative performed in conjunction with interventional radiology

Dr Robertson offers minimally invasive surgical techniques for fibroid removal and will discuss which option best suits your fibroid characteristics, symptoms, and fertility wishes.

When to seek prompt medical attention
• Sudden onset of severe pelvic pain
• Heavy bleeding soaking a pad or tampon every hour, or bleeding with dizziness/fainting
• Signs of anaemia — such as significant fatigue, breathlessness, or a racing heart
• New or worsening urinary retention
Related procedures:MyomectomyHysterectomyHysteroscopy

Abnormal Bleeding

Irregular, heavy, or post-menopausal bleeding — causes and assessment

Abnormal uterine bleeding describes menstrual bleeding that falls outside what's expected in terms of frequency, regularity, duration, or volume. This includes periods that are heavier or longer than usual, bleeding between periods, and bleeding after intercourse.

Once you have gone through menopause (12 months without a period), any vaginal bleeding is considered abnormal and warrants prompt assessment, regardless of how light it is.

Common symptoms
  • Heavy menstrual bleeding (soaking through pads/tampons, passing large clots, or bleeding lasting more than 7–8 days)
  • Bleeding or spotting between periods
  • Bleeding after intercourse
  • Irregular cycles — periods coming closer together or further apart than usual
  • Any bleeding after menopause
  • Symptoms of anaemia — fatigue, light-headedness, breathlessness — from ongoing heavy loss
What causes it?

There are many possible causes, and the right investigations depend on your age, symptoms, and risk factors. Common causes include:

  • Structural causes — fibroids, polyps, adenomyosis
  • Hormonal causes — anovulation, perimenopause, thyroid dysfunction, polycystic ovary syndrome
  • Bleeding disorders
  • Medication effects — including hormonal contraception and anticoagulants
  • Infection
  • Precancerous or cancerous changes of the endometrium or cervix — a key reason prompt assessment matters, particularly after menopause
How it's diagnosed

Assessment typically includes a history and examination, pelvic ultrasound, and blood tests (such as a full blood count, iron studies, and thyroid function). Depending on findings, further investigation may include:

  • Hysteroscopy — direct visualisation of the uterine cavity, often combined with a biopsy or dilatation and curettage (D&C)
  • Endometrial biopsy — sampling the uterine lining, particularly important if there are risk factors for endometrial cancer
Treatment options

Treatment is tailored to the underlying cause and your goals (including whether you wish to preserve fertility).

Medical management

  • Tranexamic acid to reduce blood loss during periods
  • Hormonal treatments — the combined pill, progestogens, or the hormonal IUD (often highly effective for heavy bleeding)
  • Iron replacement if you are anaemic

Procedural and surgical options

  • Hysteroscopic polypectomy or myomectomy, where polyps or fibroids are contributing
  • Endometrial ablation — a day-case procedure to thin the uterine lining and reduce bleeding, for those who have completed their family
  • Hysterectomy, reserved for cases where other treatments haven't worked or aren't suitable

Dr Robertson will work with you to identify the underlying cause and find a management approach that fits your symptoms, health, and quality of life.

When to seek prompt medical attention
• Any bleeding after menopause
• Bleeding heavy enough to soak a pad or tampon every hour for more than two hours
• Bleeding with significant dizziness, fainting, or a racing heart
• Bleeding in pregnancy
Related procedures:HysteroscopyHysterectomy

Ovarian Cysts

Fluid-filled sacs on the ovary — what they mean and how they're managed

Ovarian cysts are fluid-filled sacs that form in or on an ovary. They are very common, and the vast majority are benign (non-cancerous) — many resolve on their own without any treatment.

Cysts come in several types. Functional cysts (follicular or corpus luteum cysts) form as a normal part of the menstrual cycle and usually resolve within a few cycles. Other types — such as dermoid cysts, endometriomas, or cystadenomas — do not resolve spontaneously and may need monitoring or surgical management depending on their features.

Common symptoms
  • Many ovarian cysts cause no symptoms at all and are found incidentally on ultrasound or examination
  • Pelvic pain or a dull ache on one side, which may come and go with your cycle
  • Bloating or a feeling of pelvic fullness/pressure
  • Pain with intercourse
  • Sudden, severe pain — can indicate cyst rupture or ovarian torsion (twisting), which needs urgent assessment
  • Irregular periods, in some cases
What causes it?

Most cysts relate to normal ovulation. Others are associated with conditions such as endometriosis (endometriomas) or arise from cells within the ovary itself (such as dermoid cysts, which contain tissue like hair, skin, or fat).

The risk of a cyst being cancerous is low overall, but increases with age, particularly after menopause, and with certain ultrasound features or elevated tumour marker levels.

How it's diagnosed

Ovarian cysts are diagnosed with a pelvic ultrasound, which assesses the size, appearance, and characteristics of the cyst.

  • Blood tests — such as CA-125, and in some cases a broader panel — may be used to help assess the likelihood of malignancy, particularly in postmenopausal women or where ultrasound features are complex
  • Further imaging such as MRI may be used if ultrasound findings are indeterminate

Your individual risk is assessed using a combination of your age, menopausal status, ultrasound findings, and blood results.

Treatment options

Surveillance

  • Many simple cysts, particularly in premenopausal women, are safely monitored with a repeat ultrasound, as most resolve spontaneously

Surgical management

  • Laparoscopic (keyhole) cystectomy — removal of the cyst while preserving the ovary, generally preferred where fertility or ovarian function is a priority
  • Oophorectomy — removal of the affected ovary, considered where malignancy risk is higher, the cyst is very large, or ovarian tissue can't be preserved
  • Urgent surgery is required for suspected ovarian torsion, to try to preserve the ovary

Dr Robertson will explain the type of cyst you likely have, the estimated risk of malignancy, and recommend either surveillance or surgery based on your individual circumstances.

When to seek prompt medical attention
• Sudden, severe one-sided pelvic pain — this can indicate torsion (twisting) of the ovary, which is a surgical emergency
• Severe pain with nausea, vomiting, or fever
• Signs of internal bleeding — such as dizziness, fainting, or rapidly worsening pain, which can occur with cyst rupture
• If any of the above occur, please attend your nearest emergency department
Related procedures:Ovarian surgery

Abnormal Cervical Screening Tests

Understanding your result and what colposcopy involves

Cervical screening tests detect the Human Papillomavirus (HPV), the virus responsible for almost all cases of cervical cancer. In Australia, cervical screening has moved from the old Pap smear to primary HPV testing, done every 5 years for most people from age 25 to 74.

If HPV is detected, the same sample is examined further ('reflex' or 'liquid-based cytology') to look at the cervical cells themselves. Cervical screening allows early detection and treatment of cell changes before they ever have the chance to develop into cancer.

Common symptoms
  • Cervical cell changes and early HPV infection typically cause no symptoms at all — this is why routine screening (rather than waiting for symptoms) is so important
  • Some people with more advanced changes may notice bleeding after intercourse, bleeding between periods, or unusual discharge — these symptoms should always be assessed regardless of your last screening result
What causes it?

Almost all cervical abnormalities are related to persistent infection with high-risk strains of HPV — most commonly HPV 16 and 18, though there are other high-risk types. HPV is extremely common and most sexually active people will be exposed at some point; most infections clear on their own without ever causing problems.

If a high-risk HPV infection persists, it can cause changes in the cells of the cervix. These are graded as low-grade (often resolve on their own) or high-grade (higher likelihood of progressing if untreated, so generally warrant treatment).

How it's diagnosed

If your screening test detects HPV, the laboratory will report whether cervical cells look abnormal, and if so, whether the changes are low-grade or high-grade. Depending on this result, you may be referred for colposcopy.

  • Colposcopy is a simple procedure performed in the rooms, similar to having a cervical screening test, but Dr Robertson uses a specialised magnifying instrument (colposcope) to closely examine the cervix
  • A biopsy — a small sample of cervical tissue — is often taken during colposcopy to confirm the degree of any changes
  • The procedure is quick and generally well tolerated; some mild cramping or spotting afterwards is normal
Treatment options

Low-grade changes

  • Often simply monitored with repeat testing, as many resolve on their own

High-grade changes

  • Treatment is generally recommended to remove the affected cells before they have a chance to progress
  • LLETZ (large loop excision of the transformation zone) — the most common treatment, performed under local or general anaesthetic in the rooms or as a day procedure
  • Follow-up testing after treatment to confirm the abnormal cells have been fully cleared

Dr Robertson will explain your specific result, what it means, and the recommended next steps — whether that's reassurance and routine screening, closer monitoring, or treatment.

When to seek prompt medical attention
• Unexpected bleeding after intercourse or between periods
• Bleeding after menopause
• Persistent, unusual vaginal discharge
• Please don't wait for your next scheduled test if you notice new symptoms — contact the rooms for review
Related procedures:Cervical surgery

Vulval Disorders

Conditions affecting the vulva — assessment and treatment

The vulva refers to the external female genital area, including the labia, clitoris, and the opening of the vagina. A number of conditions can affect the skin and tissue of the vulva, ranging from infections and dermatological (skin) conditions to chronic pain disorders and precancerous changes.

Vulval symptoms are common but often go unreported due to embarrassment — these conditions are very manageable, and there is no need to feel uncomfortable discussing them.

Common symptoms
  • Itching, burning, or stinging
  • Redness, swelling, or skin changes such as thinning, whitening, or thickening
  • Pain during intercourse or with touch (vulvodynia)
  • A visible lump, ulcer, or lesion
  • Changes in skin texture or colour that persist or worsen over time
  • Pain with sitting or wearing tight clothing, in some chronic pain conditions
What causes it?

Vulval disorders have a wide range of causes, including:

  • Skin conditions — such as lichen sclerosus, lichen planus, or eczema/dermatitis
  • Infections — including thrush, herpes simplex virus, and other sexually transmissible infections
  • Chronic pain conditions — such as vulvodynia, where pain occurs without an identifiable skin change
  • Cysts or benign lumps — such as Bartholin's cysts
  • Precancerous changes (vulval intraepithelial neoplasia) or, less commonly, vulval cancer
How it's diagnosed

Dr Robertson will take a detailed history and perform a careful examination to help identify the likely cause. A biopsy — a small tissue sample taken under local anaesthetic in the rooms — is sometimes needed to confirm a diagnosis, particularly for persistent or unusual-looking skin changes.

Treatment options

Skin conditions

  • Topical treatments — such as potent topical steroids for lichen sclerosus or lichen planus, used according to a structured regimen
  • Emollients and simple measures to reduce irritation (avoiding soaps, tight clothing, and known irritants)
  • Regular follow-up, as some conditions (like lichen sclerosus) carry a small long-term risk of skin changes and benefit from ongoing monitoring

Chronic pain conditions

  • Multidisciplinary management including pelvic floor physiotherapy, topical or oral medications, and pain psychology support

Structural or benign lesions

  • Drainage or marsupialisation for Bartholin's cysts
  • Excision or biopsy of persistent or concerning lesions

Dr Robertson will work with you to reach an accurate diagnosis and put together a treatment plan aimed at relieving your symptoms and addressing the underlying cause.

When to seek prompt medical attention
• A new lump, ulcer, or skin change that doesn't heal within a few weeks
• Significant pain, swelling, or fever suggestive of a vulval abscess
• Any unexplained bleeding from a vulval lesion
• Please arrange review promptly for any persistent or concerning changes, rather than waiting for a routine appointment

This information is general in nature and does not replace individual advice from your specialist. Please discuss your specific situation, test results, and treatment options with Dr Robertson.
